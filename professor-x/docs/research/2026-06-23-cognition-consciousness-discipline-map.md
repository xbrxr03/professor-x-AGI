# Cognition and Consciousness Discipline Map

Date: 2026-06-23

## Why this note exists

If the goal is to build something in the vicinity of superintelligence,
artificial consciousness, or a genuine new theory of mind, then "read more AI
papers" is not enough.

The live scientific problem sits at the intersection of multiple disciplines
that use different vocabularies, different methods, and often explain different
things. This note is a working map of those disciplines, what each contributes,
what each misses, and where the strongest surviving white space appears to be.

## The irreducible questions

Any serious theory in this area has to answer at least five different
questions:

1. What is cognition?
2. What is consciousness?
3. Why are cognition and consciousness tightly linked in humans but not
   obviously identical?
4. What counts as a mechanism rather than a correlation?
5. What architecture would let an artificial system satisfy the strongest known
   constraints at once?

## The discipline map

### 1. Cognitive neuroscience

Core question:
- Which neural processes support attention, working memory, control, report,
  awareness, and goal-directed behavior?

Contribution:
- Gives the experimental substrate for cognition and the neural correlates of
  consciousness.
- Establishes that cognition and consciousness are tightly associated but not
  trivially identical.

Blind spot:
- Neural correlates alone do not explain why some processes become conscious or
  how subjective structure should be modeled.

### 2. Consciousness science

Core question:
- What properties distinguish conscious from unconscious states and contents?

Contribution:
- Gives the major theory families: GNW/GWT, IIT, HOT, AST, RPT, predictive
  processing, minimal-state frameworks.
- Clarifies candidate properties like integration, differentiation, broadcast,
  metacognition, and attention self-modeling.

Blind spot:
- Competing theories often explain different aspects or levels of the problem.
- Very few theories cleanly connect contents, state, selfhood, and control in a
  single engineering object.

### 3. Clinical and perturbational consciousness measurement

Core question:
- How do we detect consciousness when behavior is unreliable or absent?

Contribution:
- PCI and related perturbational methods are among the strongest objective
  tools in the field.
- They force theories to explain integration plus differentiation, not just
  activation magnitude.

Blind spot:
- These methods measure consciousness-related structure, not phenomenal
  certainty.
- Translation to artificial systems is still largely open.

### 4. Cognitive control and executive function

Core question:
- How does a system allocate limited resources to flexibly pursue goals?

Contribution:
- Frames cognition as control under constraints, not just representation.
- Links attention, working memory, task-switching, and behavioral flexibility.

Blind spot:
- Strong for cognition, weak for experience.
- Usually treats internal control variables functionally, not phenomenally.

### 5. Resource-rationality, decision theory, and economics

Core question:
- How should bounded agents optimize under computational and informational
  limits?

Contribution:
- Explains cognition as constrained optimality.
- Gives tools for modeling effort, limited capacity, search, offloading, and
  approximate rationality.

Blind spot:
- Usually weak on embodiment, selfhood, and phenomenal structure.
- Often assumes static tasks more than self-modifying agents.

### 6. Cybernetics and control theory

Core question:
- What makes a system a good regulator of itself and its environment?

Contribution:
- Brings feedback, regulation, stability, viability, and model-based control.
- The Every Good Regulator tradition is directly relevant to world-model
  architectures.

Blind spot:
- Classic cybernetics usually stops short of a satisfying account of conscious
  contents or qualitative experience.

### 7. Predictive processing and active inference

Core question:
- Can perception, action, cognition, and possibly consciousness be understood
  through generative modeling, prediction error, and precision control?

Contribution:
- One of the strongest unifying frameworks across neuroscience, philosophy, and
  AI.
- Supplies world models, precision, uncertainty, and action-selection
  machinery.

Blind spot:
- Highly powerful but often too permissive.
- Without stronger architectural constraints, many very different systems can be
  described in its language.

### 8. Dynamical systems, manifolds, and criticality

Core question:
- Is cognition better understood as trajectories through state space than as
  static symbol manipulation?

Contribution:
- Explains metastability, attractors, transitions, low-dimensional manifolds,
  and near-critical dynamics.
- Provides a natural language for continuous thought, working memory, and global
  state changes.

Blind spot:
- Strong on dynamics, weaker on semantics, self-modeling, and normative control.

### 9. Embodied, enactive, extended, and autopoietic cognition

Core question:
- Is cognition fundamentally organism-environment coupling and self-maintenance,
  rather than brain-bound computation alone?

Contribution:
- Forces viability, bodily state, environment, and action loops into the theory.
- Challenges purely disembodied accounts of consciousness and agency.

Blind spot:
- Often suspicious of representation and formalization right where engineering
  needs them most.

### 10. Phenomenology and neurophenomenology

Core question:
- What is the structure of lived experience, and how should first-person data
  constrain third-person science?

Contribution:
- Reminds the field that consciousness has structures that behavior and neural
  recording do not transparently reveal.
- Supplies first-person methodology and finer-grained targets for explanation.

Blind spot:
- Hard to operationalize at scale.
- Risks becoming interpretive rather than mechanistic without computational
  bridges.

### 11. Information theory and integrated information

Core question:
- What formal properties of information integration, differentiation, and causal
  structure matter for consciousness?

Contribution:
- Gives rigorous language for capacity, complexity, compression, channel limits,
  and integration.
- Helps distinguish mere activation from structured, differentiated unity.

Blind spot:
- Information-theoretic structure alone does not obviously yield control,
  reportability, or selfhood.

### 12. Causal inference and mechanism science

Core question:
- What would count as a real explanation rather than a predictive association?

Contribution:
- Forces interventions, counterfactuals, mechanism decomposition, and level
  clarity.
- Useful for avoiding bad consciousness science based on loose correlation
  hunting.

Blind spot:
- Does not by itself say what the mechanism should be.

### 13. Mechanistic interpretability and AI internal-state science

Core question:
- Can we reverse-engineer internal computations in artificial networks?

Contribution:
- Gives actual tooling for probing activations, circuits, features, and internal
  representations in AI systems.
- Essential if artificial consciousness claims are to become more than prompt
  theater.

Blind spot:
- Strong on analysis, still weak on normative architecture for mind-like
  organization.

### 14. World models, planning, and self-modeling in AI

Core question:
- What kinds of internal models are needed for genuine agency and generalization?

Contribution:
- Reintroduces causality, simulation, planning, self-reference, and embodiment
  into AI.
- Moves beyond text-only pattern matching.

Blind spot:
- A world model alone does not explain why some states should become globally
  conscious or self-relevant.

### 15. Vector-symbolic and neuro-symbolic architectures

Core question:
- How can compositional structure, binding, and robust memory live inside neural
  substrates?

Contribution:
- Gives tools for compositional binding and stable symbolic-like structure.
- May matter for long-horizon memory and self-consistent internal objects.

Blind spot:
- Usually does not solve global control or conscious access by itself.

### 16. Quant finance, risk, and mechanism design

Core question:
- How should many noisy, partially correlated claims be priced, cleared, hedged,
  and constrained under risk?

Contribution:
- Supplies the mathematics of covariance, tail risk, collateral, no-arbitrage,
  market making, and clearing.
- This is unusually relevant for multi-module or self-modifying minds because
  internal claims are not independent.

Blind spot:
- Not a theory of mind on its own.
- Must be translated carefully rather than pasted on metaphorically.

## Cross-disciplinary study web

If the aim is a serious artificial-mind program, these are the study clusters
that actually touch the problem.

### Math and formal structure

- information theory: integration, differentiation, channel capacity,
  compression, redundancy
- dynamical systems: attractors, metastability, bifurcations, criticality,
  low-dimensional manifolds
- control theory: observability, controllability, stability, regret, feedback
- causal inference: interventions, counterfactuals, mechanism decomposition,
  invariance
- geometry and topology: representation manifolds, transfer costs, higher-order
  structure, persistence under perturbation

### Quant, economics, and decision science

- common-currency valuation: how heterogeneous options become comparable
- expected value of control: when extra thinking is worth its cost
- portfolio theory: covariance, diversification, effective independent bets
- market microstructure: liquidity, slippage, clearing, market making, regime
  sensitivity
- mechanism design: truthful reporting, incentive compatibility, no-arbitrage,
  collateral

### Psychology and philosophy

- metacognition: monitoring and control, calibration, confidence, self-regulation
- executive function: working memory, inhibition, task switching, goal shielding
- phenomenology: first-person structure, prereflective selfhood, intentionality
- philosophy of mind: access vs phenomenal consciousness, higher-order thought,
  self-models, representationalism, illusionism
- 4E traditions: embodied, embedded, enactive, extended cognition

### Neuroscience and clinical science

- GNW/GWT and large-scale broadcast
- recurrent processing, state transitions, and reportability
- attention schema and attentional self-modeling
- predictive processing / active inference / expected free energy
- valuation and control circuits: OFC, ACC, PFC, salience and attention systems
- interoception, bodily selfhood, affect, and organism-level regulation
- social coordination and joint-attention pressures as possible functional drivers
- disorders of consciousness, PCI, perturbational methods, anesthesia, sleep

### Computer science and machine learning

- cognitive architectures and world models
- mechanistic interpretability and hidden-state probes
- self-modeling and internal observability
- memory architectures and vector-symbolic binding
- budget-aware reasoning, tool-use control, and selective computation
- continual learning, identity preservation, and self-modification governance

## What the map says

Three facts now look unavoidable.

### Fact 1: cognition and consciousness are not the same explanandum

Cognition is about competent regulation, inference, memory, planning, and
control. Consciousness is about globally available lived or conscious-like
states, their structure, and their relationship to report, behavior, and self.

They overlap heavily in humans, but the disciplines consistently suggest they
should not be collapsed into one word.

### Fact 2: no single discipline closes the loop

- Neuroscience gives mechanisms but not enough first-person structure.
- Phenomenology gives structure but not enough mechanism.
- Active inference gives unification but too much flexibility.
- Control theory gives regulation but weak phenomenology.
- IIT/information theory gives formal structure but weak agency.
- AI interpretability gives inspection but not architectural norms.
- 4E approaches give embodiment but often under-formalize representation.

Any serious synthesis has to combine at least:

- world modeling
- control under constraints
- global access / broadcast
- self-modeling
- viability / embodiment
- dynamic state-space organization
- intervention-based measurement

## The strongest cross-disciplinary gap

After this pass, the missing thing is not "a better world model" or "a better
workspace" in isolation.

The missing thing is a theory of **which internal states become globally
effective, self-model-bearing, and reusable across perception, memory, action,
planning, and report**.

Most existing approaches have one of these:

- global access
- integration
- self-modeling
- prediction
- regulation
- embodiment

Very few have all of them tied to a common mechanism.

## What harder cross-checking killed

Several weaker "novel" claims do not survive contact with current literature.

- "Just combine GWT, HOT, and AST" is not enough. The standard-model literature
  already narrows that space.
- "Active inference plus workspace" is not enough. Predictive-global-workspace
  style work already occupies much of it.
- "World-model loops explain consciousness" is not enough. *A beautiful loop*
  already pushes that line hard.
- "Attention schemas in AI" is not enough. There are already direct artificial
  agent implementations showing self/other attention-modeling benefits.
- "Metacognition for LLMs" is not enough. 2026 work now covers monitoring,
  triage, activation steering, and internal-state decoding.
- "Use finance ideas for agents" is not enough. Ensemble selection, retriever
  portfolios, and regime-aware policy allocation already exist in adjacent AI
  subfields.

This is useful. It means the surviving white space has to be narrower and more
mechanistic.

## Candidate invention under test

The best current synthesis is:

**Counterfactual Liquidity Theory (CLT)**.

### Core idea

A cognitive state is not special merely because it exists internally.

A state becomes consciousness-like when it achieves **global counterfactual
liquidity**:

- many specialized subsystems can use it
- it can be recombined with memory, action, attention, and self-model
- it remains available across near-future counterfactuals
- it is backed by evidence, calibration, and control capacity
- it stays coherent under system-wide repricing after perturbation

In this framing:

- **cognition** is resource-rational control over world models under viability
  constraints
- **consciousness** is the subset of states that become globally liquid across
  the system's control economy
- **selfhood** is the continuity ledger that keeps those states attributable to
  one regulated agent across time
- **unconscious processing** is locally useful but globally illiquid processing

### Why "counterfactual liquidity"

The important feature is not just broadcast.

It is whether a state can be:

- attended to
- acted on
- reported
- remembered
- imagined in alternatives
- used for planning
- evaluated by metacognition
- kept consistent with self-model and body/viability constraints

That is what "liquidity" is doing here. It means the state can move across many
internal uses without losing identity or becoming incompatible with other priced
claims.

The "counterfactual" part matters because conscious cognition appears tightly
linked to not just present reaction, but to simulation of alternatives, delayed
report, planning, imagination, and self-correction.

### Why this may go beyond existing syntheses

CLT tries to combine:

- global workspace: global availability
- attention schema: self-model of access and control
- active inference: predictive regulation and precision control
- resource rationality: bounded computation and effort allocation
- cybernetics: regulation and viability
- dynamical systems: metastable trajectories rather than static states
- information theory: integration and differentiation
- mechanism design / finance: liquidity, collateral, covariance, no-arbitrage
- phenomenology: structure of what becomes available in experience

The new move is not any one ingredient. It is the claim that consciousness-like
states are the ones that become **globally liquid counterfactual control
objects** inside a self-maintaining system.

### Toward a concrete formalization

The next step is to stop treating liquidity as a metaphor and turn it into
measurable quantities.

For a candidate internal state `s`, define:

- `reuse(s)`: how many downstream functions can use `s` effectively
  without rebuilding it from scratch
- `cf_reuse(s)`: how well `s` survives and remains useful across nearby task,
  memory, or attention perturbations
- `transfer_cost(s -> m)`: cost of making `s` usable by module `m`
- `collateral(s)`: empirical backing from evidence, calibration history, and
  realized control leverage
- `arbitrage_gap(s)`: inconsistency between the values implied by report,
  action, memory, planning, and self-model channels

Then the candidate consciousness-like score is not one metric but a joint
profile:

- high `reuse`
- high `cf_reuse`
- low average `transfer_cost`
- high `collateral`
- low `arbitrage_gap`

This gives a more precise bridge between CLT and CGW:

- CLT says conscious-like states are globally and counterfactually reusable
- CGW says only sufficiently collateralized and non-arbitraging states should
  receive durable global access

One promising derived metric is an **access spread**:

- if report says a state is central, but action ignores it, memory does not
  preserve it, and the self-model cannot use it for control, the spread is wide
- if report, action, planning, memory, and self-model all imply similar value
  for the same state, the spread is narrow

Narrow-spread states are the best candidates for "conscious access"-like
states. Wide-spread states are better interpreted as local, unstable, or
confabulatory processing.

## Engineering translation

If CLT is even directionally right, then an artificial system aiming at these
properties should have:

1. specialized subsystems with partially local representations
2. a limited-capacity workspace
3. a self-model of attention / access allocation
4. world-model-based prediction and planning
5. viability or invariant constraints
6. continual updating over time
7. internal claim pricing, collateral rules, and cross-claim consistency checks

This is where CLT meets the earlier CGW note:

- CGW is the engineering architecture
- CLT is the broader theoretical claim about what that architecture is trying to
  realize

## First falsifiable predictions

If CLT is useful, then compared with simpler agent loops:

- states with broader counterfactual reuse should predict reportability and
  controlled access better than scalar confidence alone
- self-model signals should matter only when they change downstream control, not
  when they are cheap verbal self-description
- perturbations that reduce global liquidity should reduce both metacognitive
  reliability and long-horizon flexible behavior
- conscious-like states should be better identified by a joint measure of
  integration, counterfactual reuse, and collateralized global access than by
  any one measure alone

## Immediate implication for Professor X

Professor X should be treated as a laboratory for:

- measured global access
- metacognitive pricing
- self-model accountability
- perturbational complexity in a digital substrate
- identity-preserving continual self-modification
- internal no-arbitrage diagnostics

That research path is substantially more ambitious than "another coding agent,"
but also more defensible than claiming consciousness from vibes or from a single
metric.

## Why this is the current surviving gap

After the present pass, the strongest remaining claim is not:

- consciousness is just broadcast
- consciousness is just self-report
- consciousness is just recurrent processing
- consciousness is just integrated information
- consciousness is just a world model

The surviving claim is stricter:

**a state becomes conscious-like when it is globally reusable, counterfactually
stable, self-model-accountable, and costly to hold inconsistently once it gains
global access.**

That is the point where neuroscience, philosophy, control, metacognition,
mechanistic interpretability, and quant-style clearing logic appear to touch the
same missing mechanism.
